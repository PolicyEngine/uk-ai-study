\section{Introduction}

Artificial intelligence is expected to affect household incomes through three distinct channels. First, AI can substitute for human labour in tasks it performs more cheaply, displacing some workers from their jobs---the automation channel long studied for earlier waves of technology \citep{autor2003, acemoglu2025}. Second, for workers who remain employed, AI can act as a complement, raising productivity and hence wages; aggregate estimates suggest such productivity effects could be substantial \citep{briggs2023}. Third, AI raises the returns to the capital embodied in and financed by the technology, boosting profits, dividends and interest income for those who own assets \citep{cazzaniga2024}. Because workers at risk of displacement, workers who gain from complementarity, and owners of capital occupy different positions in the income distribution, the net effect of AI on inequality is an empirical question that depends on the relative size of each channel and on who is exposed to it.

This report examines that question for the United Kingdom. The UK is a natural case study: its economy is unusually services-intensive, and services occupations---professional, financial, administrative and managerial work---score highly on standard measures of AI exposure \citep{felten2021, eloundou2023, pizzinelli2023}. Estimates for advanced economies consistently place the UK among the countries with the largest shares of employment in AI-exposed occupations. At the same time, the UK operates a strongly redistributive tax and benefit system, so the ultimate effect of any AI shock on disposable incomes, poverty and inequality depends not only on where the shock lands in the labour market but on how taxes and transfers reshape it. Capturing that interaction requires microsimulation.

Causal evidence on AI's labour market effects remains scarce and recent, and no consensus estimate exists for the size of displacement, wage or capital effects over the coming years. We therefore follow a scenario-based approach rather than a forecasting one. We define plausible ranges for each of the three channels, drawing on the emerging empirical literature, and simulate a grid of scenarios covering their combinations. The output is not a prediction of what AI will do to UK incomes but a mapping of what it could do under transparent assumptions---and, importantly, an identification of results that hold across the entire scenario space.

Our framework replicates the study of Ireland by \citet{doorley2026}, published jointly by the ESRI and the Irish Department of Finance, with three substitutions dictated by the UK setting. In place of the SWITCH model we use PolicyEngine UK (v2.89.2), an open-source microsimulation model of the UK tax and benefit system. In place of the Irish SILC 2022 we use the Family Resources Survey 2024--25, the standard microdata source for UK income and tax-benefit analysis. And in place of two-digit ISCO-08 occupations we assign AI exposure at the level of SOC2020 major groups recorded in the FRS adult file, using occupational exposure measures from the literature \citep{felten2021, tolan2021, eloundou2023}. As in the Irish study, exposed workers face a probability of displacement, surviving workers in exposed occupations receive a wage premium, and returns to household capital income rise; the tax-benefit model then translates these market income shocks into disposable income, poverty and inequality outcomes.

We also extend the framework in one direction the original study does not pursue: age. Early evidence on generative AI adoption points to a strikingly seniority-biased pattern of employment effects. Using UK firm-level data, \citet{kleinteeselink2025} finds that firms exposed to generative AI have cut employment by around 4.5 per cent, with junior roles falling by 5.8 per cent. In US resume data, \citet{hosseini2026} document a decline in junior employment of roughly 9 per cent at AI-adopting firms within six quarters of adoption, with senior employment essentially unaffected. And \citet{brynjolfsson2025} show a relative employment decline of about 13 per cent for workers aged 22--25 in the most AI-exposed US occupations. Motivated by this evidence, we add an age dimension to our exposure mapping and implement each of these three studies as an explicit displacement scenario, asking how a youth-tilted shock changes the fiscal and distributional consequences relative to age-neutral displacement. This matters for the UK debate because younger workers sit differently in the household income distribution, interact differently with the benefit system, and face a longer horizon over which scarring effects can compound \citep{williamson2024}.

The remainder of the report proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature on AI exposure measurement and emerging evidence on labour market effects. Section 3 describes the data, the mapping of exposure scores to SOC2020 major groups, and the PolicyEngine UK simulation framework. Section 4 sets out the scenario design, including the central calibration, the full scenario grid, and the age-differentiated scenarios. Section 5 presents results: the distributional profile of exposure, and the effects of each scenario on government revenue and spending, poverty, and inequality. Section 6 concludes with policy implications for retraining and lifelong learning and for the resilience of the tax base as income shifts from labour to capital.
