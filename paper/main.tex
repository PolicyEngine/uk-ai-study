\title{Who bears the AI shock? Displacement, poverty and the public finances in the UK\thanks{Draft --- not for citation.}}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent I study how the household and fiscal costs of an AI labour-market shock in the United Kingdom depend on who bears it. Occupation-level AI exposure is linked to workers in the Family Resources Survey 2024--25, and the implied employment, wage and capital-income shocks are passed through PolicyEngine UK, an open-source tax--benefit microsimulation model, across 66 shock-size combinations and five incidence families that hold the aggregate shock fixed while varying who is displaced. In the central scenario---7 per cent of employees displaced---the annual Exchequer cost is \pounds 18.4 billion and before-housing-costs poverty rises by 1.87 percentage points. Reallocating the same aggregate shock moves the fiscal cost from \pounds 14.2 billion under uniform incidence to \pounds 24.5 billion under expertise compression, and \pounds 32.5 billion under a family shaped by measured UK firm-level displacement patterns, while poverty changes far less. Income inequality rises in every scenario. The incidence of the shock determines whether it arrives as a fiscal problem or a poverty problem.
\end{abstract}
