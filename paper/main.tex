\begin{abstract}
\noindent How much an AI labour shock costs the United Kingdom depends on who bears it. We build the first open-source, full tax-benefit microsimulation of AI displacement incidence for the UK: occupation-level AI exposure is attached to Family Resources Survey workers, shocks are simulated across 66 scenarios, and counterfactual incomes are evaluated with PolicyEngine UK, treating the incidence of displacement --- who loses work, not just how many --- as an explicit scenario axis. In the central scenario (7 per cent displacement, 2.6 per cent wage gain for remaining workers), the annual Exchequer cost is \pounds 18.4 billion, before-housing-costs poverty rises by 1.87 percentage points and the Gini by 1.10 points. Holding the aggregate shock fixed, the fiscal cost varies from \pounds 14.2 billion (uniform incidence) to \pounds 24.5 billion (expertise compression); a fifth family shaped by measured UK firm-level displacement patterns is costlier still, at \pounds 32.5 billion. Among simulated policy responses, a temporary Universal Credit uplift is the most cost-effective poverty stabiliser, at \pounds 10.4 billion per percentage point of after-housing-costs poverty averted, against \pounds 12.8--14.3 billion for wage insurance. If half the displaced wage bill reappears as taxed corporate profits, the net revenue shortfall falls from \pounds 21.5 billion to \pounds 11.8 billion, and a constituency-level mapping locates the sharpest income losses in Northern Ireland and parts of London. Who bears the shock decides whether the UK gets a fiscal problem or a poverty problem; inequality rises either way, in every cell and every family.
\end{abstract}
